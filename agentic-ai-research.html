01 — Core thesis

A structural shift in consumer decision-making

We are no longer building tools that help people decide. We are building systems that decide on their behalf. This transition — from AI as copilot to AI as autopilot — introduces a critical, underexplored tension in consumer psychology.

The core trade-off: high AI autonomy reduces cognitive load and decision fatigue, but can trigger psychological reactance when consumers feel displaced from the process. This dynamic reaches its peak at the most consequential moment: the payment.

Central research question: When consumers delegate financial decisions to AI, what determines whether they feel empowered or overridden — and how do those perceptions drive or inhibit final transaction execution?

Existing theoretical frameworks were built for the left side of this spectrum. This research develops the tools needed to understand the right.

02 — Research gaps

Three disconnected streams,
one missing bridge

Existing literature approaches this problem from isolated disciplines. None fully captures what happens when AI moves beyond influencing decisions to executing them on behalf of consumers.

Stream 01
Autonomy psychology
Self-determination theory establishes autonomy as a fundamental human need — but only in human-only decision contexts. No framework accounts for AI-delegated decisions.
Stream 02
AI systems research
Emphasises automation, efficiency, and multi-agent coordination. Rarely asks how the consumer experiences a system acting on their behalf.
Stream 03
Consumer behaviour
Studies algorithm aversion and AI-assisted choice — but primarily at recommendation stages. Final decision execution remains largely unstudied.

The missing mechanism: What drives a consumer to execute a payment after AI-assisted selection? How does perceived autonomy shift that final conversion — and does it change when AI is delegated authority rather than a supporting role?

03 — Theoretical framework

An integrated model of
AI-mediated decision-making

This research bridges three theoretical domains that have operated in silos — producing the first integrated framework for consumer behaviour under agentic AI delegation, connecting psychological theory with AI systems and behavioural processes.

Pillar 01
Psychological theory
Autonomy as a fundamental need · self-determination theory · psychological reactance · perceived control
Pillar 02
AI systems
Agentic AI capabilities · decision delegation · multi-agent coordination · autonomy spectrum
Pillar 03
Behavioural processes
System 1 vs System 2 thinking · conversion dynamics · uniqueness neglect · decision quality
01
Reconceptualising autonomy
Autonomy is reframed as a dynamic, context-dependent construct — not binary control, but a perceived role in the process that shifts across journey stages.
02
The delegation threshold
Identifies the psychological tipping point at which consumers move from AI-assisted to AI-delegated — and the conditions that make delegation feel empowering vs threatening.
03
Conversion-stage autonomy
Extends prior literature to the final execution stage: how autonomy perception at the payment moment determines whether intent converts to action.
04
Uniqueness neglect mechanism
Explains how perceived autonomy compensates for AI's failure to capture individual preferences — reducing reactance and improving decision quality.

COMMERCE

Insights on progressive autonomy and the delegation threshold directly inform how "buy-on-behalf" AI systems should be structured to maximise consumer adoption without triggering reactance.
Google · OpenAI · Visa · Mastercard
NEOBANKS
